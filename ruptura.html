RUPTURA DA MATRIZ

2025

LAMP CREATED USING MARQUETRY

During my final semester of college, I developed the "Ruptura" lamp in the strategic design practice.

The idea behind this work was to reinterpret an ancestral technique. Since then, I've conducted extensive research to discover a technique that would make sense to explore differently within my portfolio.

I chose marquetry as the technique for my project. Marquetry is an ancient art of creating designs and patterns on surfaces, usually wood, by inlaying pieces of materials such as woods of different colors, metals, mother-of-pearl, and stones, forming decorative mosaics.

The technique involves cutting precise shapes and assembling them with patience and skill, resulting in furniture, paintings, and objects of high aesthetic value, ranging from geometric designs to complex figures.

Each artisan has a cultural background in marquetry techniques inherited from their family, but every artisan also applies a significant amount of personal experience in each piece.

During my research, I felt a strong desire to work with something related to street culture, especially graffiti, which carries a strong personal aesthetic, similar to the artisans of ancestral marquetry.

I researched artists who could represent this concept effectively, such as Edmun, an artist from Belo Horizonte internationally recognized for his work. Within this theme, I felt his visual language fit perfectly with the project.

That said, I created the concept of "rupture of the matrix," inspired by the idea of breaking ancestral patterns without losing the connection to marquetry traditions.

I developed the lamp heavily inspired by Edmun's graffiti. From different perspectives of the same lamp, the veneer collage references the artist's graphic style.

I would like to thank Professor Camilo Belchior and Professor Maria Flávia who assisted me during the process, and I also leave here an invitation to explore Edmund's exceptional work.

ORIGIN AND HISTORICAL CONTEXT

The technique originated during the Edo Period (16001868) in the Hakone region of Kanagawa Prefecture, Japan.

This area is the birthplace of Yosegi-zaiku and remains its main center today. Since 1984, Yosegi-zaiku has been recognized by the Japanese government as an important cultural heritage craft.

Modern artisans such as Kiyotaka Tsuyuki, from the fourth generation of the Tsuyuki Mokkousho workshop, work to preserve the tradition while adapting it to contemporary contexts.

Muku: In this technique, different types of wood are combined and glued together to form a geometric patterned block, which is later sculpted into three-dimensional objects.

Zuku: This technique creates patterned wooden blocks that are sliced into extremely thin sheets, almost like paper, and applied to decorative surfaces.

Marquetry in Brazil has distinct characteristics that differ from traditions such as Japanese Yosegi-zaiku, especially through the use of native materials and artistic inspirations connected to Brazilian culture.

TOPOMORPHOSIS

Topomorphosis by designer Heloísa Crocco uses the growth rings of trees and textures of Amazonian wood as inspiration for prints and patterns.

Crocco researches artisanal techniques focused on reuse and the application of natural patterns inspired by biodiversity.

The project extends into industry and community collaborations, connecting art and everyday life through exhibitions and product lines.

Observation of Nature: the artist researches growth rings and transforms these natural marks into visual patterns and textures.

Creation of Matrices: from wood grain studies, she creates high-relief matrices used as the foundation for her artistic production.

Reproduction on Different Media: these matrices are printed on paper, fabrics, metals, and other surfaces, generating organic geometric mosaics.
